Volume Profile: Explanation and Interpretation Using Al Brooks' Methodology

How Volume Profile Works:

  • Volume Distribution: Volume Profile displays the amount of trading activity (volume) that has occurred at various price levels over a selected time period. Unlike traditional volume bars, which show volume for each time period, Volume Profile shows volume across price levels, giving a horizontal view of volume.
  • Components:
    • Point of Control (POC): The price level where the most volume has been traded. This is often where the market has found the most equilibrium.
    • Value Area: Represents the range where approximately 70% of the volume has been traded, indicating where the market has spent most of its time.
    • High Volume Nodes (HVNs): Price levels with significantly high volume, often acting as support or resistance.
    • Low Volume Nodes (LVNs): Areas with relatively low volume, where price might move quickly if it breaks through due to lack of historical interest.

 

Using and Interpreting Volume Profile with Al Brooks' Methodology:

  1. Market Phase Identification:
    • Trading Ranges: If Brooks points out that the market is unclear, Volume Profile would show a dense concentration of volume around certain price levels, suggesting a trading range. Here, HVNs indicate where the price has historically been "comfortable," potentially showing where the market might return if it's not trending.
    • Trends: In a trend, the Volume Profile might show a more dispersed pattern, with volume increasing at new highs or lows, confirming the trend's strength. A clear breakout on high volume at or near new price levels would align with Brooks' view on breakouts.
  2. Breakouts:
    • Confirmation: Brooks notes that breakouts are the strongest part of a trend. Volume Profile can confirm these by showing increased volume at new price levels. If a breakout occurs but isn't accompanied by significant volume at the breakout level, it might be less reliable.
    • POC and Value Area: If the price breaks out of the value area, particularly past the POC, it's a strong indication of a trend continuation, supporting Brooks' advice to trade with the trend.
  3. Reversal Attempts and the 80% Rule:
    • Volume at Reversals: When Brooks talks about the 80% rule where most reversal attempts fail, Volume Profile can indicate where these attempts might occur.     • Failed Breakouts: Volume Profile can highlight where previous breakouts have failed, often around LVNs, which supports Brooks' warning against trading against the trend based on low probability scenarios.
  4. Entry and Exit Points:
    • Buying Pullbacks: In a strong bull trend, Brooks suggests buying on pullbacks. Volume Profile can show where these pullbacks might find support (HVNs), offering entry points with a higher probability of trend continuation.
    • Exiting Trades: When deciding to exit, look for price moving into or past significant volume areas like the POC or the edges of the value area. This could signal potential exhaustion or a significant support/resistance level where the market might react.
  5. Risk Management:
    • Stops: Place stops below or above significant volume areas, particularly around HVNs, to manage risk effectively, as these levels are where the market has shown interest.
    • Position Sizing: If the volume profile suggests a breakout with wide volume distribution, consider smaller positions due to the potentially larger stops needed, aligning with Brooks' risk management principles.
  6. Psychological Context:
    • By showing where the market has been active, Volume Profile reduces uncertainty, helping traders align their psychology with market reality, which is key in Brooks' approach. It can confirm trends or trading ranges, reducing the likelihood of counter-trend trades.

In summary, Volume Profile enriches Al Brooks' price action methodology by providing visual cues about where traders have been active, thus offering insights into potential support, resistance, and trend confirmation or denial.
